OceanSolvency.com — Concept Note

This note sketches how OceanSolvency.com can be used as a descriptive, neutral .com banner for ocean-related physical risks with potential solvency implications for financial institutions, ports, infrastructure investors and coastal territories. It is not a rating, not an official label and not investment advice.

1. Core idea: “ocean solvency” as a missing language

In most organisations, ocean-related risks are scattered across climate, physical risk, catastrophe modelling, shipping, ports, coastal infrastructure, fisheries and regional planning. Yet, at Board and supervisory level, the question is simpler:

“What do rising seas, coastal erosion and ocean disruption mean for our solvency, capital and long-term viability?”

OceanSolvency.com provides a single, exact-match phrase for that question:

  • linking ocean and coastal hazards to balance sheets,
  • framing ocean-related shocks as solvency drivers, not just environmental issues,
  • offering a banner under which data, models and policies can be organised.

2. Strategic context (2025–2035)

Over the coming decade, several trends increase the relevance of an “ocean solvency” lens:

  • Sea-level rise and coastal flooding affecting ports, coastal real estate and industrial zones.
  • Storm surge and erosion driving higher damage, downtime and infrastructure adaptation costs.
  • Ocean warming, acidification and ecosystem stress impacting fisheries, tourism and coastal economies.
  • Maritime disruption (shipping routes, congestion, chokepoints) with direct implications for trade, supply chains and revenues.

For banks, insurers, reinsurers, infrastructure funds, ports and coastal authorities, these dynamics feed into:

  • capital requirements and risk appetite,
  • asset valuations and stranded-asset risk,
  • financing and insurance conditions in coastal regions.

OceanSolvency.com is designed as a public-facing name for positioning this agenda at executive and investor level.

3. Potential acquirers & positioning

The domain is primarily relevant for actors who need to tell a coherent story about ocean-related solvency risk, for example:

  • Banks and insurers with material coastal and maritime exposure,
  • Reinsurers and specialty insurers focused on marine and coastal risk,
  • Port groups, shipping alliances and logistics/terminal owners,
  • Infrastructure and real-asset funds with coastal portfolios,
  • Public agencies, development banks or coalitions working on coastal resilience and finance.

The strength of the name is its role as a neutral, descriptive category, not as a narrow product brand.

4. Illustrative use cases (within clear limits)

4.1 Possible directions

  • Ocean solvency framework for a group:
    • mapping coastal and ocean-related exposures,
    • connecting hazard scenarios to solvency views,
    • linking adaptation and resilience investments to risk metrics.
  • Coastal & ocean data platform:
    • bringing together hazard layers, assets, counterparties and financial data,
    • providing dashboards and indicators under the “ocean solvency” banner,
    • supporting internal committees and external disclosure.
  • Ocean & solvency observatory:
    • tracking stress in key coastal and maritime regions,
    • publishing non-binding indicators, case studies or scenarios,
    • supporting alliances between public and private actors.
  • Capital and risk narrative:
    • explaining how ocean-related risk is integrated into pricing, underwriting, lending or investment policies,
    • positioning the organisation as an early mover on this theme.

4.2 Clear limits

  • the domain itself does not confer any official status,
  • it should not be presented as a regulatory label or rating,
  • any “ocean solvency” metrics or views remain the sole responsibility of the acquirer and their advisers.

The domain is a banner and communication asset. All methodologies, assumptions and claims made under it are under the acquirer’s control and responsibility.

5. Relationship to wider “solvency” themes

Ocean-related risk rarely stands alone. Many organisations are exploring broader “physical solvency” questions around:

  • climate and transition risk,
  • water stress,
  • nature and biodiversity,
  • energy systems and infrastructure.

OceanSolvency.com can sit alongside other descriptive assets focused on climate, water, nature, energy or planetary solvency, as part of an integrated naming strategy.

In that stack, OceanSolvency.com captures the ocean and coastal dimension, while remaining compatible with broader climate and risk frameworks.

6. Legal, regulatory and communication considerations

Any use of the domain should recognise that:

  • it does not represent a public authority or regulator,
  • it does not replace legal, regulatory or supervisory guidance,
  • it should avoid creating confusion with official labels or ratings.

This concept note:

  • is illustrative and non-binding,
  • does not constitute legal, financial, scientific or investment advice,
  • does not provide any opinion on the solvency of any entity or instrument.

The acquirer should work with their own legal, regulatory, risk-management and communications experts to define appropriate language and disclosures.

7. Transaction & contact

A transaction around OceanSolvency.com would typically follow:

  • NDA and clarification of intended use and governance,
  • formal offer and agreement on price and conditions,
  • escrow process and transfer of the OceanSolvency.com domain name.

Only the domain name is transferred. No products, models, services or advisory work are included by default.

All present and future uses, statements and claims made under this banner remain the sole responsibility of the acquiring entity and its advisers.
